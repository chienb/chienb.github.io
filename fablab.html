Fabrication Lab Web Design

CSUDH

2017

Generating interest through a landing page with meaningful and informative images

The Challenge/Problem

The Fabrication Lab (Fablab) is a department within CSUDH which brings Science, Technology, Engineering and Mathematics (STEM) to the forefront of K-16 education through leadership and service. One of the most important projects is the fleet of mobile fabrication labs. These trucks tow around customized mobile trailers outfitted with the state-of-the-art fabrication equipment to classrooms and communities, providing and inspiring young engineers and inventors with hands-on experience.

The Fablab needed an appealing landing page that would generate interest when users browse the website and ultimately encourage them to submit an application for the trailers to visit their school/community.

Approach

It was clear that the community was interested in the project when it was first announced, but the public had no idea what the trailer or equipment looked like. It's hard to get excited about something you can't see or touch (yet). Therefore, it made sense to show images of the trailer with a brief description for each of the fabrication equipment. This will allow kids to look up and research the equipment before visiting the mobile Fablab. They will have the opportunity to prepare questions and have them answered through a hands-on experience by the staff.

Design

Typography

I picked only 2 typefaces to keep the interface consistent and simple. I chose Open Sans for the body elements because it is easy on the eyes. Sans serif typefaces, which lack the ligatures appended to the end of serif strokes, are often easier to read on the web. The shapes are much simpler and easier to comprehend.

It wasn't easy trying to find the typeface for heading elements. I was looking for a futuristic and tech-oriented font which will fit the context of Fablab. So I browsed fontpair.co to look for inspirations. I was finally able to settle on 2 options, Ubuntu or Raleway. I chose Raleway because it was originally designed for headings and other large size usage.

Wireframe

I created a rough wireframe to kickstart some ideas. I like to do this because it helps me visualize the flow of different layouts from a top-down view. Sometimes we get caught up in the small details we lose vision of the bigger picture. The introduction block is the section for your "elevator pitch" - a clear statement or image of what your website is about. This area will be mostly information, so I included a brief description of the Fablab and some stats to add a little more depth.

The next section is what I like to call "features". We see a lot of these on the landing pages of ecommerce websites. They usually highlight the features of the products with big callouts and images; the nitty-gritty details that will entice you to buy the product. In our case, the fabrication equipment are the "features" of the mobile Fablab product.

The supplementary block is an area for any additional information you would like to include on the website. This would be a great section to include a gallery or testimonial to re-engage the user to perform a specific action such as register, donate, purchase, etc.

The end block usually contains the CTA element and the footer of the website. For our project, we want to thank our sponsors for their support - by displaying their logos above the footer section.

Reflection

Nothing is more true than the phrase "a picture paints/is worth a thousand words", even in web design. Pictures have the power to create an emotional response in the audience, faster than text. A properly designed website can manipulate the audience to feel whatever it wants them to feel.

The new Fablab landing page was able to generate a lot of buzz and interest in the community through meaningful and informative imagery.
